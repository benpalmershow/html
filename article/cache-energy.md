# CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + Δ

Calcium oxide (quicklime) reacts with water in a violently exothermic release — up to 1,155 kJ per kilogram of CaO. The reaction is simple, ancient, and reversible. Heat calcium hydroxide back above 512°C and you recover the oxide and the water. What you store is not electrons in lithium or pumped uphill in a reservoir. You store energy in a chemical bond that sits at ambient temperature indefinitely, loses nothing to self-discharge, and costs almost nothing per unit of stored heat.

## The Thermodynamics

CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + 65.2 kJ/mol

The reverse:

Ca(OH)₂ + heat → CaO + H₂O

The round-trip efficiency hovers around 70-80% depending on insulation and recovery method. Compare to lithium-ion batteries at 85-95% but with degradation over 10-15 years, or pumped hydro at 70-85% but requiring geography. Calcium hydroxide stores heat at 400-600°C in a powder that sits in a bag on a shelf. No charge cycles. No capacity fade. No fire risk from dendrites. It does not care if you use it tomorrow or in thirty years.

## Scale

Global quicklime production is roughly 350 million tonnes per year. The infrastructure to produce, transport, and handle CaO is mature — it is one of the oldest industrial chemicals on Earth, used in cement, steel, water treatment, and agriculture. The supply chain exists. The material is abundant. Limestone (CaCO₃) is the most common sedimentary rock on the planet. You calcine it at 900°C to get CaO, then you hydrate it to store energy, then you calcine it again to release. The cycle is:

1. **Charge**: Apply heat (solar thermal, industrial waste heat, nuclear) to Ca(OH)₂ → CaO + H₂O
2. **Store**: CaO sits inert at ambient temperature for hours, days, months, years
3. **Discharge**: Add water → Ca(OH)₂ + heat (up to 500°C steam)

## Applications

**District heating.** Store summer solar thermal. Discharge in winter for building heat. No battery, no grid dependency, no seasonal storage penalty.

**Industrial process heat.** Cement, glass, ceramics, and chemical manufacturing need temperatures between 300-900°C. Current solutions burn natural gas. Thermochemical storage provides dispatchable heat from intermittent renewable sources.

**Grid-scale backup.** Not for electricity directly — for heat. 70% of global energy demand is thermal, not electrical. Store the heat chemically, convert to steam turbine power when needed.

**Spatial and temporal arbitrage.** Charge at point A (where energy is cheap or abundant), discharge at point B (where heat is needed). Transport the material, not the energy.

## The Catch

The reaction causes particle attrition — repeated cycling breaks down the granules, reducing reactivity over hundreds of cycles. Researchers mitigate this with structural supports (Al₂O₃, TiO₂ matrices) or by operating with larger particle beds where surface area loss is manageable. The energy density by mass (~1 MJ/kg for the reaction itself) is lower than hydrocarbons (~45 MJ/kg for natural gas), so you need volume. But you also need nothing else — no pipeline, no combustion chamber, no emissions controls, no carbon tax.
