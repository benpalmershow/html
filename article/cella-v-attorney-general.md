# Cella v. Attorney General: Rent Control Initiative Blocked on Religious Exemption Grounds

**Case:** Cella v. Attorney General, SJC-13893  
**Decided:** June 23, 2026  
**Court:** Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts

---

## Core Holding

The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court unanimously held that Initiative Petition 25-21 — a rent control measure that would limit annual rent increases to the lesser of CPI or 5% — **cannot appear on the November 2026 ballot** because its exemption for "dwelling units in facilities operated solely for... religious... purposes" causes the petition to "relate to religion" in violation of Article 48 of the Massachusetts Constitution.

---

## The Initiative

**Initiative Petition 25-21** sought to:
- Repeal G. L. c. 40P, which bans rent control statewide
- Replace it with a cap on annual rent increases at the lesser of the Consumer Price Index or 5%
- Apply to all "covered dwelling units" leased for residential use

**Exemptions included:**
- Owner-occupied buildings with four or fewer units
- Units regulated by a public authority
- Transient guest units (less than 14 days)
- **Units in facilities operated solely for educational, religious, or non-profit purposes**
- Units with a certificate of occupancy less than 10 years old

---

## The Legal Challenge

Four registered voters sued to block the petition, arguing the religious exemption violated Article 48's excluded matters provision, which states:

> "No measure that relates to religion, religious practices or religious institutions... shall be proposed by an initiative petition."

The Attorney General had certified the petition as compliant, but the court reviewed that certification de novo.

---

## The Court's Reasoning

**Majority Opinion (Gaziano, J.)**

The court applied the plain language of Article 48 and its historical context:

1. **"Relates to religion" standard:** An initiative relates to religion if religion is "a factor in [the initiative's] application." The religious exemption makes religion exactly that — a factor in determining which units are covered by rent control.

2. **Precedent from Collins v. Secretary of the Commonwealth (1990):** The court relied on its earlier holding that a law altering the rights and obligations of religious institutions cannot be the subject of a referendum because it "make[s] religion a factor in the application of the exemption" and "alter[s] the legal status of religious institutions."

3. **Secular exemptions do not cure the defect:** The presence of secular exemptions (educational, non-profit) does not negate that the petition uses religion as a factor in its application.

4. **"Main purpose" test rejected:** The Attorney General argued the petition's main purpose (limiting rent increases) was secular. The court declined to apply the "main purpose" test to the religion exclusion, distinguishing it from the "powers of courts" exclusion. Unlike the courts exclusion, applying the religion exclusion according to its plain language does not threaten to make the initiative process a nullity — proponents can simply avoid drawing distinctions based on religion.

5. **Historical intent:** The 1917-1918 constitutional convention delegates intended to strictly separate church and state and "make it as difficult as possible to bring religious questions into the politics of this State."

**Concurring Opinion (Kafker, J.)**

Justice Kafker concurred on narrower grounds, focusing on the specific religious test the exemption creates:

- The exemption requires determining whether a dwelling unit is operated "solely for... religious... purposes"
- This invites "intrusive review of religious beliefs and practices"
- It requires inquiry into "the internal affairs of, and even the motives and purposes of, a religious institution, as well as its finances"
- Examples include religious retirement communities, universities, and sober houses
- Whether such a unit is operated "solely" for religious purposes demands a "deep dive" into both finances and religious practices

Justice Kafker noted that Massachusetts is unique among States in having such a religion exclusion in its initiative process.

---

## Key Precedents Cited

- **Article 48 of the Massachusetts Constitution** — Excluded matters provision
- **Collins v. Secretary of the Commonwealth**, 407 Mass. 837 (1990) — Law altering religious institution exemptions related to religion under Article 48
- **Opinion of the Justices**, 309 Mass. 555 (1941) — Contraceptive care initiative did not relate to religion because it concerned secular public health
- **Mazzone v. Attorney General**, 432 Mass. 515 (2000) — "Main purpose" test for judicial powers exclusion

---

## Practical Effect

The court remanded the matter to the county court for:
1. Entry of judgment declaring Initiative Petition 25-21 noncompliant with Article 48's excluded matters section
2. Enjoining the Secretary of the Commonwealth from placing the measure on the November 2026 statewide election ballot

Rent control proponents must now either:
- Redraft the initiative without any religious exemption, or
- Pursue the issue through the Massachusetts Legislature

---

## Broader Implications

**For Initiative Drafting:** The decision signals that any initiative containing a religious exemption — even in an otherwise secular measure — risks being barred from the ballot under Article 48.

**For Church-State Separation:** The ruling reinforces Massachusetts' strict separation of church and state as reflected in both Article 48 and the anti-aid amendment (Article 46).

**For Rent Control Debate:** The immediate effect is removal of the rent control measure from the 2026 ballot, delaying any statewide rent control effort until at least 2028.
