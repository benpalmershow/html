# Chatrie v. United States: Geofence Searches and the Fourth Amendment

**Case:** Chatrie v. United States  
**Docket:** 25-112  
**Argued:** 2026  
**Decision Expected:** June/July 2026

---

## Core Legal Question

Whether the **warrantless use of a geofence search** — where law enforcement obtains location data from Google for all devices within a defined geographic area during a specific time window — violates the **Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures**.

---

## What Is a Geofence Search?

A geofence search allows law enforcement to:
- Define a geographic area (the "geofence")
- Specify a time window
- Obtain location data from Google for **all devices** that were within that area during that time
- Identify potential suspects without a warrant or individualized suspicion

This technique has become increasingly common in criminal investigations, particularly for crimes like burglary, robbery, and vandalism where physical proximity to the crime scene is relevant.

---

## Constitutional Issue

**The Fourth Amendment** requires that searches be reasonable. Traditionally, a search required:
1. A warrant supported by probable cause, OR
2. A recognized exception to the warrant requirement

The government argues geofence searches fall under the **third-party doctrine** — that individuals have no reasonable expectation of privacy in information voluntarily shared with third parties (like Google, which collects location data for its services).

**Petitioner's argument:** Modern location tracking reveals intimate details of life, and the third-party doctrine should not apply to the comprehensive, continuous surveillance that geofence searches enable.

---

## Precedents Likely to Shape the Decision

### Carpenter v. United States (2018)
Held that cell-site location information (CSLI) requires a warrant. Established that historical location data is protected by the Fourth Amendment.

### Riley v. California (2014)
Required a warrant to search a cell phone during an arrest. Recognized that modern digital devices contain vast personal information.

### Katz v. United States (1967)
Established the "reasonable expectation of privacy" test. Information voluntarily shared with third parties may lose Fourth Amendment protection.

### United States v. Jones (2012)
GPS tracking of a vehicle constitutes a search under the Fourth Amendment.

---

## Arguments Structure

### Petitioner (Chatrie) Position

**Geofence searches are unconstitutional because:**
- They constitute general rummaging through private digital history
- No individualized suspicion for hundreds/thousands of devices swept
- Chilling effect on movement and association
- Google acts as unwilling government informant with massive data trove
- Third-party doctrine doesn't apply to comprehensive, warrantless surveillance

### Respondent (United States) Position

**Geofence searches are permissible because:**
- Location data is voluntarily shared with third parties (third-party doctrine)
- The search is limited in time and space — not a general warrant
- Investigative technique less invasive than physical search
- Good faith reliance on existing legal authority
- Necessary for modern law enforcement in the digital age

---

## Key Themes to Watch During Oral Argument

#### 1. Digital Privacy Expectations
- How does the Court frame privacy expectations in location data?
- Does geofence data reveal "intimate details" of life?
- Distinction between voluntary sharing and pervasive surveillance

#### 2. Scope and Scale
- How many devices can be searched without a warrant?
- What limits should exist on geographic and temporal scope?
- Can the government conduct multiple overlapping geofence searches?

#### 3. Technological Understanding
- Will justices grasp geofencing mechanics?
- Expect multiple "what if" hypotheticals testing boundaries:
  - Smaller vs. larger geographic areas
  - Longer vs. shorter time windows
  - Different data providers (Google vs. others)

#### 4. Practical Implications
- Effect on criminal investigations and digital evidence collection
- Role of private sector (Google's data retention policies)
- Implications for other digital surveillance techniques

---

## Potential Outcomes

### Ruling for Chatrie (Petitioner Wins)
- Geofence searches require a warrant
- Strong Fourth Amendment protection for location data
- Law enforcement must use traditional methods (witnesses, cameras, other evidence)
- May require legislative solution for regulated geofence access

### Ruling for United States (Government Wins)
- Geofence searches permissible without warrant under third-party doctrine
- Broad latitude for digital investigation techniques
- Enhanced law enforcement tools but reduced privacy protections
- Possible call for legislative regulation instead

### Narrow Ruling
- Court crafts case-specific test
- May uphold some geofence uses while limiting others
- Leaves broader questions for future cases

---

## Justice Questioning Patterns to Watch

**Conservative bloc:** May defer to law enforcement but emphasize property/trespass principles.

**Liberal bloc:** May expand privacy rights beyond Carpenter, concerned about government surveillance.

**Swing justices:** Likely to probe practical effects, notice-and-consent frameworks, and whether alternative methods exist.

---

## Broader Implications

This case will determine:
- **Digital privacy scope** in the age of continuous location tracking
- **Third-party doctrine viability** as technology evolves
- **Law enforcement capabilities** for investigating crimes in digital environments
- **Balance between security and liberty** in the 21st century

The ruling will affect not just geofence searches but also other emerging surveillance techniques that rely on data collected by private companies.

---

## Amendments Referenced

- **Fourth Amendment:** Protection against unreasonable searches and seizures

---

## What's at Stake

If the Court rules for Chatrie:
- Law enforcement will need warrants for geofence searches
- Location data receives heightened Fourth Amendment protection
- Similar digital surveillance techniques may be restricted

If the Court rules for the United States:
- Warrantless geofence searches become a standard investigative tool
- Broad expansion of government access to digital data without judicial oversight
- Potential for widespread use across thousands of investigations annually

---

**Stakes:** This case will shape the future of digital privacy and define the limits of government surveillance in an era where our movements are constantly tracked by private companies.
