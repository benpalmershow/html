Chiles v. Salazar

Speech, Conduct, and Viewpoint Discrimination in Professional Regulation

Legal Analysis • October 7, 2025

Analysis of the Supreme Court oral argument examining whether Colorado's ban on sexual orientation and gender identity change efforts is a viewpoint-based speech restriction or permissible regulation of harmful medical conduct.

Key Takeaways from Oral Argument

Petitioner's Position

Colorado's statute is a content-and-viewpoint-based restriction on speech that "prophylactically bans voluntary conversations" and categorically prohibits certain counseling goals for minors, triggering strict scrutiny.

Government's Counter

The law regulates medical or professional conduct (harmful treatment) rather than pure speech—a health-and-safety regulation aimed at harmful practices not subject to typical First Amendment strict scrutiny.

Solicitor General's Position

The law is viewpoint-and-content-based and outside historical exceptions. The record lacks evidence showing non-aversive, licensed talk therapy to minors is harmful.

Judicial Fault Lines

Multiple Justices probed whether therapy is only speech (protected) or speech integral to medical conduct (regulable), with repeated concerns about viewpoint discrimination.

Why Viewpoint Discrimination Matters

If the law is correctly characterized as viewpoint-based censorship—allowing counseling that advances one stance (gender-affirming) while forbidding counseling that advances the opposite stance (assistance aiming to reduce or alter sexual orientation or gender identity)—then it is subject to strict scrutiny and will survive only if narrowly tailored to a compelling state interest.

Multiple parts of the transcript frame the statute as literally producing that asymmetry. The government's framing—that the statute targets treatment/conduct because certain practices are medically harmful—would place the regulation under a different doctrinal lens: regulation of professional medical practice and patient safety, which historically gives states substantial regulatory room.

The Speech vs. Conduct Distinction

The transcript revolves around a precise distinction:

  • If medical conduct: The regulation can be upheld as a permissible professional-practice rule under the state's police powers to regulate healthcare and protect minors.
  • If speech: Classic First Amendment protections attach, and the state must satisfy strict scrutiny or show the speech is unprotected (e.g., fraud, incitement, or speech integral to unlawful conduct).

Colorado's counsel repeatedly distinguished "help me become straight" (banned) from "help me cope" (permitted), emphasizing the statute's focus on the purpose of changing identity or orientation rather than the content of conversations about identity.

How States Justify These Bans

States rely on several overlapping justifications:

  1. Statutory framing: Many state bans prohibit "sexual orientation change efforts" (SOCE) or "gender identity change efforts" (GICE) for minors, defined broadly to include attempts to eliminate or reduce same-sex attraction or alter gender identity. States frame these as targeting harmful treatment rather than protected speech.
  2. Regulatory authority: States invoke their police powers to regulate healthcare practice and protect minors from interventions deemed unsafe or ineffective. When characterized as medical treatment, courts have traditionally given states broad authority.
  3. Evidentiary claim of harm: States cite epidemiological and survey studies associating conversion/GICE practices with adverse mental-health outcomes (suicidality, depression). Colorado's counsel pointed to such studies; petitioners and the SG disputed whether those studies isolate licensed, non-aversive talk therapy to minors.
  4. Professional consensus: States rely on official positions from major professional organizations condemning these practices as ineffective and harmful.

Professional Organizations' Positions

The professional consensus is that attempts to change sexual orientation or gender identity are not established, evidence-based treatments and are associated with harms. These official positions form the backbone of state regulatory justifications.

Practical Doctrinal Outcome Paths

Three potential frameworks the Court may adopt:

1. Viewpoint-Based Speech Restriction → Strict Scrutiny

If the Court treats the law as viewpoint-based speech restriction, the state must show a compelling interest and narrow tailoring. The transcript records skepticism that Colorado's evidentiary record meets that bar.

2. Regulation of Medical Conduct → Deferential Review

If the statute is categorized as professional-practice regulation aimed at preventing harmful treatments, the state has a much stronger path to uphold the ban. Colorado pressed this framing repeatedly.

3. Middle Path / Hybrid Approach

The Court could limit its ruling to the specific configuration before it (licensed talk therapy to minors) or adopt a test distinguishing speech-only counseling from speech-integrated medical procedures—a nuanced rule that would leave room for state regulation of medicalized care while protecting pure conversational therapies.

How the Transcript Uses (and Contests) Professional Positions

Respondents' Reliance

Colorado's counsel invoked studies (Green; Turban; agency reports) and professional assessments to argue the statute targets harmful clinical practices, not protected speech.

Petitioner & SG Pushback

They argue the record lacks studies specifically about licensed, non-aversive talk therapy to minors—the exact conduct petitioner engages in. The evidentiary record does not prove the kind of direct causation required to survive strict scrutiny if the statute is treated as speech regulation.

Analytical Summary

The oral argument centers on a straightforward doctrinal hinge: is the Colorado ban policing medicine/healthcare conduct, which states may regulate, or is it policing speech and viewpoint inside the counseling room, which triggers strict First Amendment protection?

The transcript, the SG's briefing, and professional associations' statements supply competing pressures: professional bodies' consensus that SOCE/GICE are ineffective and harmful gives the state a policy rationale; but the absence (according to petitioner and the SG) of record-level evidence about non-aversive, licensed talk therapy to minors cuts against upholding a categorical speech ban under strict scrutiny.
