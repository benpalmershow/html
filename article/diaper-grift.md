# Diaper Grift

The press release tells you the feel-good story. Free diapers for needy families! The numbers tell the feel-bad reality. Only 'participating' hospitals. Only Baby2Baby diapers. Only one route for taxpayer dollars to flow: from the state budget, to a nonprofit's overhead, to a single vendor's warehouse, to a limited set of families.

The budget tells you everything.

---

## The Unit Economics

| Line Item | FY25-26 | FY26-27 |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Diaper manufacturing cost | $3.8M | $7.6M |
| Warehouse & logistics | $0.8M | $1.6M |
| Direct program costs | $1.6M | $2.2M |
| RFI exploration (Phase 2 commercial distribution) | $0.5M | — |
| State operations | $0.7M | $1.1M |
| **Total** | **$7.4M** | **$12.5M** |

80 million diapers ÷ $12.5 million = **$0.156 per diaper** in direct acquisition.

But the program doesn't stop at acquisition. It layers warehousing, state administration, and "direct program costs" that absorb another $5.3 million over two years. The effective cost per delivered diaper, when you include bureaucracy, is **~$0.50** — higher than Amazon's retail price for a comparable product.

---

## The Private Alternative

Amazon sells Pampers Swaddlers 108-count for **$45.99** delivered. That's **$0.426 per diaper** with:
- Choice of brand and size
- Home delivery
- No eligibility verification
- No nonprofit overhead
- No state employee salaries
- No feasibility studies for phase two

A restricted-use voucher or direct cash transfer would cost less per family helped, empower parents to choose the diaper that fits their child, and eliminate layers of middlemen. But that would allocate resources through individual preference rather than bureaucratic contract.

---

## Baby2Baby's Balance Sheet

[Form 990 filings](https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/organizations/464503539/202543219349326759/full) show the organization's financial structure:

- **COO compensation:** $239,742
- **Net revenue from gala:** $2.4 million
- **Net assets:** $70.4 million
- **In-kind donations:** 614

The group's [board and "angels"](https://baby2baby.org/our-board-angels/) consists of LA philanthropists and entertainment executives. The annual gala generates millions in revenue that sustains the organization's operations and salaries. When taxpayer money enters this model, it flows through a well-funded nonprofit apparatus that excels at fundraising but not at minimizing overhead.

---

## Why This Pattern Persists

State-administered voucher programs don't generate gala revenue. They don't create donor recognition opportunities. They don't employ COs at $240,000. They simply transfer purchasing power to families.

Outsourcing to a nonprofit solves the political problem of "handing out cash" while creating an administrative ecosystem that requires:
- Contract management
- Reporting requirements
- Monitoring and compliance
- Brand stewardship

Each layer adds cost. The result: $0.50 per diaper when the market delivers the same product at $0.43 with choice and convenience built in.

---

## The Real Allocator

Private capital allocation means price discovery, competition, and innovation driving down costs while expanding choice. State-administered charity means appropriation, bureaucracy, and contracting driving up costs while narrowing options.

California could simply give families a restricted-use benefit card loaded with the equivalent budget. Parents buy diapers where they shop, choose brands that work, and avoid the warehousing, nonprofit salaries, and state operations that consume 40% of the budget.

Instead, the state chose a model that benefits:
- The nonprofit's fundraising narrative
- The vendor's negotiated contract
- The board's social capital
- The bureaucracy's budget justification

Every stakeholder except the one who actually needs the diaper.

---

## Bottom Line

- **Total program cost:** $0.50+ per diaper
- **Private market cost:** $0.43 per diaper
- **Administrative overhead:** ~40% of budget
- **Choice:** Zero — families receive what Baby2Baby provides
- **Transparency:** Budget documents obscure real unit costs

The state could have achieved better outcomes at lower cost through direct transfer. It chose administration instead.

That's not charity. It's a grift — and the families getting the diapers are the last ones to benefit from the money spent in their name.
