The Berkeley professor is distraught becuase[^fb1] he's no longer the sole expert on Fake.
The story is a story. The narration is the story. There is quaint dialogue that's made up. There are succinct conclusions and large ommissions[^fb2] (does this professor own 100 acres in VT and a multimillion dollar[^fb10] Berkeley Hills mansion and have zero financial concerns that afford him time to talk to a friendly NYT reporter for an anti-tech diatribe?) It's not news. We can decide on that. But it's on the front page, above the fold; and we are warned of yet another existential threat. 

Framing liberal expertise as the savior, academics feel threatened by new democratizing technology. They are warning us that we should feel threatened (not empowered). 
Becuase[^fb3] they are scared (I am too), they retreat becuase[^fb4] they are cowards.
They can afford to retreat. The farm in VT with 100 acres is just available. Not to you, dear reader. You will have to fend for yourself.

The image of this professor using a chainsaw is real. But the story is fake becuase it purports to be genuine. The photo is edited. So, is that something we should dread? Are we expert enough to notice that the backkground is artificially blurred. "I saw that forest with my own eyes and none of it was blurred!" There is no perspiration on his shirt. "Is he stacking the wood later after the reporter leaves?" His pants have no accumulated caked sawdust. Where are the pieces of bark and debris? Did he just start work? His shoes are brand new. His body has no upper body muscle. His face isn't grimaced because he most likely cut once, the photographer Erin tapped her iPhone (maybe a 'real' camera because she's a 'real' reporter) with a badge that says PRESS.The photo tells the real story. He hasn't held a chainsaw often and prefers to use his hands to type on a computer to make a living. It's a pose. It's for the camera. It's an act. It's the 'News'. We're the experts with our own eyes. This guy don't know shit![^fb5]
But we are told by cynical elites acting as experts who want to watch the world burn. 'Told you so!'[^fb6]

Fake imagery is not new. 9/11 was only real because you saw it on television. How you interpreted that day was up to the media. Everything is mediated. Before TV, there was print, before print, there was aural[^fb7]. There was the tradition. It erodes with time. 
In news, there was Cronkite. Now the TV is on a screen in your pocket and everything on that screen is encrypted by amateurs. And amareurs use their eyes do decrypt[^fb8] the signs and symbols in the stories we make up and tell ourselves. 

But the stories can be positive if we want them to be. The news doesn't need to be negative.
The youth cannot retreat. They will make up their own stories. And they will be positive because they have to be. There will be immaculate birth and resurection[^fb9]. There will be routine blessed by commandment. There has to be happily ever after. It can't be, "We're screwed."

# Editorial Notes

This poem makes provocative claims about media expertise and the democratization of information, but its impact is diminished by several mechanical errors that distract readers from the argument. The misspellings of "because" (lines 8 and 12) and "omissions" (line 9) are particularly jarring, as they appear frequently and are fundamental to the piece's credibility.

The grammar errors compound the spelling problems. "Guy don't know" (line 15) violates standard subject-verb agreement—"guy doesn't know" would be correct. Similarly, "do decrypt" (line 19) should be "to decrypt" for parallel structure with the infinitive form. The "Becuase" at the start of line 12 has both spelling and capitalization issues, as sentence-initial words must be capitalized regardless of internal errors.

Word choice needs attention. "Aural" (line 18) refers to hearing or the ear, but the context suggests "oral" tradition—communication through speech—which better fits the historical progression from oral to print culture. The hyphen in "multimillion-dollar" (line 9) is required when the compound adjective precedes the noun it modifies; this is standard NYT style.

The quoted phrase "'Told you so!'" (line 16) uses outer quotation marks unnecessarily. In the New York Times, we avoid decorative punctuation around quoted exclamations unless they are embedded within a larger sentence. Removing the outer quotes would clean up that line. For the more colorful "doesn't know shit," we might consider toning down to "doesn't know anything" or "doesn't know the subject matter" to maintain the Times' measured tone while preserving the sentiment.

## Fact-Checking and Additional Editorial Notes

The factual claims in this text would require rigorous verification before publication. The reference to 9/11 being "only real because you saw it on television" is a provocative assertion that would need attribution or at minimum, clarification that this is the author's interpretation rather than an established fact. Walter Cronkite's historical role as a trusted news anchor is accurately referenced, though the comparison between his era and today's media landscape warrants more nuance.

The Berkeley Hills mansion reference would benefit from specificity—Berkeley does have hills, but confirming whether the subject actually owns such property would be essential. The mention of "100 acres in VT" (Vermont) paired with a California location raises questions about the professor's actual geographic footprint that a fact-checker would pursue.

Structurally, the poem shifts between three thematic sections—academic retreat, media critique, and generational optimism—without clear transitions. Consider breaking these into distinct stanzas or adding connective phrasing to guide readers through the argument. The phrase "routine blessed by commandment" is opaque and may confuse readers unfamiliar with the intended religious reference.

The capitalization of "Fake" and "Fake imagery" suggests this is a proper noun or technical term in the author's lexicon, but without context, readers may wonder whether this refers to a specific concept, the general phenomenon of falseness, or is simply stylistic emphasis. Clarification or consistent treatment would help.
