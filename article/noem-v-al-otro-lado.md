# Noem v. Al Otro Lado

## Oral Argument Analysis - March 24, 2026

---

## Case Overview

**Docket:** No. 25-5  
**Argued:** March 24, 2026  
**Issue:** Whether an alien who is stopped on the Mexican side of the U.S.-Mexico border has "arrived in the United States" within the meaning of the Immigration and Nationality Act (8 U.S.C. §§ 1158(a)(1), 1225(a)(1), 1225(a)(3))

**Petitioner:** Kristi Noem, Secretary of Homeland Security  
**Respondent:** Al Otro Lado, Inc. (immigrant rights organization)

**Lower Court:** Ninth Circuit (ruled for respondents in October 2024)

---

## Background

The case centers on the "metering" policy employed by U.S. Customs and Border Protection since 2016. Under this policy, officials at U.S.-Mexico border ports of entry turned back noncitizens (including asylum seekers) to wait on the Mexican side before being processed. The policy was discontinued by the Biden administration in 2021, but the Trump administration has indicated it may reinstate it.

The Ninth Circuit held that noncitizens who present themselves at a port of entry - even if physically located in Mexico - have "arrived in the United States" under federal immigration law and must be allowed to apply for asylum.

---

## Issues at Hand

### Primary Issue
- **Statutory Interpretation:** Does "arrives in the United States" require physical presence within U.S. borders, or does presenting at a port of entry suffice?

### Constitutional Questions
- Separation of powers - Executive discretion in border management
- Treaty obligations under U.N. Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees

### Practical Implications
- Whether the government can legally turn away asylum seekers at the border
- Potential reinstatement of the metering policy
- Impact on asylum seekers waiting in Mexico

## Oral Argument Summary

The argument lasted approximately 80 minutes. Assistant to the Solicitor General **Vivek Suri** argued for the government, while **Kelsi Corkran** argued for the respondents.

### Petitioner's Position (Government)

Suri argued that "in ordinary English," a person "arrives in" a place when they are physically there - not standing at the threshold. He invoked the Court's 1993 decision in *Sale v. Haitian Centers Council*, which held that the extraterritorial application of refugee protections did not apply.

Key arguments:
- "You can't arrive in the United States while you're still standing in Mexico"
- The policy is a reasonable exercise of executive discretion
- International treaties apply to actions "within the United States," not outside

### Respondent's Position (Al Otro Lado)

Corkran argued that Congress intended to provide asylum protections to those seeking refuge at our doors, and that presenting at a port of entry constitutes "arriving."

Key arguments:
- The statute uses "arrives in" not "enters"
- Those waiting in line at a port of entry are at the threshold of the U.S.
- The government cannot condition access to asylum on illegal border crossing

## Justice Questions and Positions

### Chief Justice John Roberts
- Explored where exactly the line should be drawn
- Asked whether "how many people are in line" affects the analysis
- Appeared sympathetic to government's position that physical presence is required

### Justice Clarence Thomas
- Asked about international law and its bearing on the dispute
- Inquired whether U.N. Convention applies extraterritorially

### Justice Sonia Sotomayor
- Skeptical of government's interpretation
- Pressed on whether the policy violates the letter and spirit of refugee protections
- Noted that the government "lets in workers with permits" and other categories
- Observed: "They've arrived. They are knocking at the door."
- Expressed concern about creating a system where only those who cross illegally can access asylum

### Justice Elena Kagan
- Identified what she called a "massive superfluity problem" with government's interpretation
- If "arrives in" means must be in U.S., then "physically present in U.S." becomes redundant
- Suggested the government reading renders part of the statute meaningless

### Justice Neil Gorsuch
- Pressed on line-standing scenarios: "So anybody at the water's edge of the Rio Grande on the Mexican side has arrived?"
- Explored what it means to be at a port of entry

### Justice Brett Kavanaugh
- Noted potential inequity: those who cross illegally get asylum hearings, those who wait legally do not
- Acknowledged the case presents "a close question" on statutory interpretation

### Justice Amy Coney Barrett
- Pressed on what "arrives" means: "How close do you have to be?"
- Asked about areas without ports of entry
- Explored the line between "arrives in" vs. "arrives at"

### Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson
- Sympathetic to challengers
- Raised mootness question - since policy is no longer in effect, is there a live controversy?
- Challenged: "Your suggestion that the United States would say, 'Unless you can figure out a way to illegally cross, we're not going to entertain that claim,' seems very peculiar"
- Queried how justices have authority to review if case is moot

### Justice Samuel Alito
- Asked about "knocking at the door" analogy
- Questioned whether someone "arrives in" a country by approaching but not entering

## Analysis and Predictions

Based on argument dynamics, the Court appears likely to rule for the government:

1. **Conservative majority sympathetic** - Roberts, Alito, Thomas, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett all expressed skepticism toward respondents' reading

2. **Liberal justices divided** - Kagan identified statutory problems with government's position; Sotomayor and Jackson defended challengers

3. **Key textual argument** - Kagan's "superfluity" point may not have swayed the majority

4. **Practical concerns** - Court's recent pattern of deferring to executive on immigration matters

A decision is expected by late June or early July 2026.
