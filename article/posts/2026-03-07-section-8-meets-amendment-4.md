### <i data-lucide='scale' class='post-icon'></i> **Section 8 Meet Amendment 4**

New York just hit a constitutional wall in its voucher enforcement strategy after the Appellate Division held that forcing landlords into Section 8 conflicts with Fourth Amendment protections.

On March 5, 2026, the Appellate Division, Third Department, in *Matter of People of the State of N.Y. v Commons West, LLC* affirmed lower court rulings that struck down Executive Law section 296(5)(a)(1) to the extent it makes refusal to rent to Section 8 tenants unlawful.

The court reasoned that New York's source of income law effectively compels participation in a federal program that requires property inspections and access to landlord records through the Housing Assistance Payment contract. In the panel's view, that setup creates a constitutional problem because participation is no longer voluntary at the state level.

Attorney General Letitia James responded the same day, emphasizing that voucher holders deserve safe and dignified housing and confirming that her office is reviewing the decision.

The immediate consequence is legal uncertainty for enforcement against landlords who reject Section 8 vouchers in areas covered by this ruling. The larger consequence is a direct constitutional collision between fair housing goals and search and seizure limits.
