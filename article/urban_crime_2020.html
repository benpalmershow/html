The Bureau of Justice Statistics surveys 240,000 people across 150,000 households annually, revealing crimes that never make it to official statistics.

The Urban Crime Wave

The violent crime rate rose from 19.0 in 2020 to 23.3 in 2022, then remained stable through 2024.

Urban Violent Crime Trend: 2020-2024

The pattern shows a clear rise after 2020, followed by two years of stabilization. This suggests the initial increase may have been tied to specific events or conditions that have since plateaued.

Rural vs. Urban: Different Directions

While cities experienced this violent crime plateau, rural areas saw property crime rates decline:

Rural property crime rates declined 5.2% from 2023 to 2024, while urban violent crime increased 12% from 2020 to 2024.

Who Gets Victimized Most

Young adults aged 25-49 face the highest victimization rates at 32.1 per 1,000 persons, compared to 25.8 for ages 18-24 and 18.5 for those over 50.

Victimization Rates by Age Group

The data reveals stark disparities: young Black adults in large metropolitan areas face the highest risks during evening and nighttime hours.

The Hidden Crime Problem

58% of violent crimes in urban areas never get reported to police.

This means for every 100 violent crimes that occur in America's cities, police only learn about 42 of them. The other 58 remain hidden from official statistics.

City Size and Timing Matter

Large metropolitan areas consistently show higher victimization rates than smaller cities. The data also confirms what many urban residents already know: evening and nighttime hours carry significantly elevated risk levels.

The Bureau of Justice Statistics collects this data through the National Crime Victimization Survey, interviewing 240,000 persons age 12 or older across 150,000 households annually to capture both reported and unreported crimes.

Source: Bureau of Justice Statistics. (2024). Criminal Victimization, 2024. NCJ 310547. U.S. Department of Justice.
