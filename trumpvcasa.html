Trump v. CASA: Universal Injunctions, Not Birthright Citizenship

Examining the judicial authority to issue nationwide relief against federal policies

What Is This Case About?

Trump v. CASA de Maryland centers on the judicial authority to issue nationwide injunctions against federal policies. While the underlying policy involved immigration regulations, the key legal question concerns whether federal courts have the constitutional and equitable authority to issue relief that extends beyond the specific parties to a case, effectively blocking federal policies across the entire United States.

Contextual Background: This case emerged during a period of significant litigation challenging Trump administration immigration policies, with numerous district courts issuing nationwide injunctions that prevented policy implementation across all jurisdictions.

Key Legal Issues in Universal Injunctions

1. Universal Injunctions: Scope and Constitutional Authority

Universal (or nationwide) injunctions permit a single federal court to enjoin the government from implementing a policy against anyone, not merely the plaintiffs in the case. This remedy raises fundamental questions about the appropriate scope of judicial power in a constitutional democracy.

Arguments Supporting Universal Injunctions: "They ensure uniform application of law, prevent constitutional violations at scale, and provide complete relief when policies are found unlawful."
Arguments Against Universal Injunctions: "They exceed judicial authority, concentrate power in individual district courts, encourage forum shopping, and prevent percolation of legal issues."
Statistical Context: During the Trump administration, federal courts issued over 55 nationwide injunctions against federal policies—more than during the entire administrations of Presidents Bush and Obama combined, raising concerns about judicial overreach.

2. Article III Limitations: Constitutional Boundaries on Judicial Power

Article III, Section 2 of the Constitution states: "The judicial Power shall extend to all Cases, in Law and Equity, arising under this Constitution, the Laws of the United States, and Treaties made, or which shall be made, under their Authority..." This provision establishes and simultaneously delimits federal judicial power.

Article III's "case or controversy" requirement fundamentally constrains judicial power by limiting courts to resolving specific disputes between particular parties. This limitation raises serious questions about the constitutional basis for universal injunctions:

  • Standing Doctrine: Courts can only remedy injuries to parties who have demonstrated Article III standing
  • Remedial Principles: Traditional equity practice suggests that relief should be no broader than necessary to provide complete relief to plaintiffs
  • Separation of Powers: Universal injunctions potentially allow courts to exercise power more akin to legislative action than adjudication

The Supreme Court in Gill v. Whitford (2018) emphasized that "a plaintiff's remedy must be tailored to redress the plaintiff's particular injury." This principle challenges the constitutional foundation of relief that extends to non-parties.

3. Rule 23 and Class Actions: Procedural Safeguards and Alternatives

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 establishes the requirements for class action certification, providing a procedural mechanism to aggregate similar claims while ensuring due process protections. The relationship between class actions and universal injunctions raises important questions about procedural integrity:

Rule 23 Requirements for Class Certification:

  • Numerosity: The class must be so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable
  • Commonality: There must be questions of law or fact common to the class
  • Typicality: The claims of representative parties must be typical of class claims
  • Adequacy: Representatives must fairly and adequately protect class interests

Rule 23(b)(2) specifically addresses injunctive relief, permitting certification when "the party opposing the class has acted or refused to act on grounds that apply generally to the class, so that final injunctive relief or corresponding declaratory relief is appropriate respecting the class as a whole."

Critics argue that universal injunctions circumvent these procedural protections, particularly:

  • No formal assessment of representative adequacy
  • No notice to affected parties
  • No opportunity for potentially affected parties to opt out
  • No preclusive effect to protect the government from relitigating the same issues

4. Bills of Peace and Historical Equity Practice

The historical foundation for modern injunctive relief lies partly in equity practices that predated the Constitution. Bills of peace were equitable remedies that could occasionally bind non-parties, making them relevant to contemporary debates about universal injunctions:

Bills of Peace: These were equitable remedies that allowed courts to resolve multiple related disputes in a single proceeding when:

  • Numerous parties were involved in related controversies
  • A single resolution would prevent multiplicity of suits
  • Complete relief required binding persons beyond immediate parties

Equity Rule 48, a predecessor to modern class action rules, permitted representative suits where parties were too numerous to bring before the court. While these historical remedies did occasionally operate beyond immediate parties, their scope and application differed significantly from modern universal injunctions:

  • Bills of peace typically involved identified groups with clear relationships to plaintiffs
  • Relief against the government was extremely limited before the 20th century
  • Historical equity practice emphasized balancing competing interests rather than absolute prohibitions

Post-Civil War cases involving injunctive relief for former slaves are sometimes cited as precedent for broad equitable relief, though scholars debate whether these cases truly support modern universal injunction practice.

5. Administrative Uniformity and Policy Implementation

The practical implementation of federal policies presents significant challenges when injunctions apply piecemeal across different jurisdictions. This raises concerns about administrative burden and the uniform application of federal law:

  • Administrative Burden: When injunctions apply in some jurisdictions but not others, federal agencies face complex compliance challenges
  • Policy Percolation: Limited injunctions allow legal issues to develop in multiple courts, potentially benefiting eventual Supreme Court review
  • Winters Test Factors: Courts applying the preliminary injunction standard must balance likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and the public interest

The Supreme Court has increasingly expressed concern about universal injunctions through its "shadow docket," where emergency applications for stays of nationwide injunctions have become more common. Justice Thomas's concurrence in Trump v. Hawaii specifically questioned whether "district courts have the authority to issue universal injunctions," suggesting the Court may eventually impose significant limitations on this practice.

Scholarly Resources and Further Reading

Samuel L. Bray: "Multiple Chancellors: Reforming the National Injunction"

Professor Bray's influential article argues that nationwide injunctions contravene traditional equity practice and exceed the constitutional powers of federal courts. He traces the historical development of injunctive relief and proposes limiting injunctions to protecting named plaintiffs.

Mila Sohoni: "The Lost History of the 'Universal' Injunction"

Professor Sohoni examines historical cases where courts issued injunctions with effects extending beyond immediate parties, challenging the claim that universal injunctions lack historical precedent. She identifies instances from the early twentieth century where courts issued relief that functionally resembled modern universal injunctions.

Nicholas Bagley & Samuel Bray: "Judges Shouldn't Have the Power to Halt Laws Nationwide"

This accessible analysis explains why nationwide injunctions pose constitutional concerns and practical problems for legal development, arguing they represent a significant departure from traditional judicial practice that undermines the separation of powers.

Amanda Frost: "In Defense of Nationwide Injunctions"

Professor Frost contends that nationwide injunctions serve important functions in the modern administrative state, preserving uniform application of federal law and preventing constitutional violations. She argues that limiting injunctions to named plaintiffs can create inequities and administrative confusion.

Justice Thomas's Concurrence in Trump v. Hawaii

In this significant concurring opinion, Justice Thomas provides a detailed analysis of why he believes universal injunctions exceed Article III power and depart from historical practice. The opinion (beginning on page 49) signals the Supreme Court's growing skepticism toward this remedy.

Executive Summary: Key Insights

  • Universal injunctions raise fundamental questions about judicial power limits under Article III of the Constitution, highlighting tensions between providing effective remedies and respecting separation of powers principles.
  • The relationship between class actions (Rule 23) and universal injunctions presents important procedural considerations—class certification provides due process safeguards, while universal injunctions offer expedient remedies without comparable protections.
  • Historical equity practices, including bills of peace and Equity Rule 48, provide competing narratives about the legitimacy of broad injunctions, with scholars drawing different conclusions about whether these precedents support modern universal injunction practice.
  • Practical concerns about uniform implementation of federal policies must be balanced against constitutional limitations on judicial authority, with courts increasingly recognizing this tension in recent decisions.
  • The Supreme Court appears increasingly skeptical of universal injunctions, suggesting that future decisions may significantly restrict their availability, requiring more tailored remedies focused on specific plaintiffs or certified classes.
